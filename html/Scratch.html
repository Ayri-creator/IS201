How to Crochet

  1. Choose the Right Tools
    • Start with a medium‑weight yarn and a size H/5.0 mm hook, which are easiest for beginners to control.

  2. Learn How to Hold the Hook
    • Practice holding the hook like a pencil or knife and let the yarn flow loosely through your non‑dominant hand.

  3. Make a Slip Knot
    • Create a simple adjustable loop on the hook — this is the foundation of almost every crochet project.

  4. Practice the Chain Stitch
    • Wrap the yarn over the hook and pull it through the loop repeatedly to form a chain, the base of most patterns.

  5. Learn the Single Crochet
    • Insert the hook into a chain, yarn over, pull through, then yarn over again to complete your first real stitch.

  6. Work Your First Row
    • Continue single crocheting across the chain to build your first row of fabric and get used to stitch tension.

  7. Turn and Continue Rows
    • Chain one, turn your work, and crochet into each stitch to build consistent rows and improve muscle memory.

Beginners Crochet Glossary

  • Slip Knot
    • The foundational loop that attaches your yarn to the hook. Adjustable, secure, and the first motion every crocheter learns.
  • Chain (ch)
    • A series of linked loops that form the “spine” of most crochet projects. Used to start rows, rounds, and shapes.
  • Single Crochet (sc)
    • A tight, dense stitch. Great for amigurumi, washcloths, and anything that needs structure./li>
  • Half Double Crochet (hdc)
    • A medium‑height stitch that’s slightly taller than single crochet but still dense and beginner‑friendly.
  • Double Crochet (dc)
    • A tall, airy stitch that works up quickly. Ideal for blankets, scarves, and garments.
  • Magic Ring / Magic Circle
    • A loop that lets you start crocheting in the round with no hole in the center. Essential for hats and amigurumi.
  • Yarn Over (yo)
    • Wrapping the yarn over the hook to create new loops. Used in almost every stitch.
  • Frog / Frogging
    • Unraveling your work to fix a mistake. (Because you “rip it, rip it.”)
  • Gauge
    • How many stitches and rows fit into a measured square. Important for sizing garments and patterns.
  • Tension
    • How tightly or loosely you hold the yarn. Affects stitch size and overall fabric feel.
